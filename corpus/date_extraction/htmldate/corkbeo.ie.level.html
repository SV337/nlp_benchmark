Level 3 LIVE from Cork as Ireland adapts to incoming coronavirus restrictions

The country avoided Level 5 last night, but NPHET have warned it may be down the line

corklive
  • Share
  •  
  • Subscriptions
  • Sign Out
By
Gavin O'Callaghan
  • Updated21:22, 6 OCT 2020
News

Tonight at midnight Cork will be moved into Level 3 and some harsher restrictions will come into place.

The new rules will trigger a lot of changes and some businesses (mainly pubs and restaurants) will be shutting or changing the way they serve.

These new rules will be in place until the 27th of October, at which point the government will make another decision depending on the covid figures.

It's been a terrible start to the week, and the latest rules have come with a big fall-out between the government and the National Public Health Emergency Team who advised a Level 5 and feel the country will have to be upgraded in a few weeks if not now.

This means that sadly, there may not be a lot to look forward to at the end of these three weeks. If NPHET's ignored projection is correct there'll be harsher restrictions to come.

That said the government have argued that the numbers could drop and that could be avoided if the public comply strictly with the rules of Level 3.

We'll be covering and explaining these decisions as clearly as possibly over the day, but the main focus is how this will affect Cork locally.

There's already been a lot of frustration, especially among publicans who've only just been given clearance to open.

NPHET's letter

Ireland could see up to 2,300 new cases of coronavirus per day by November - and a drastic Level 5 lockdown is needed to stop it effectively.

That was the stark warning contained in the letter from CMO to the Health Minster before the government decided to bring the country in Level 3.

Dr Tony Holohan outlined a number of reasons the NPHET wanted to bring back harsh restrictions, and if the team are correct in their projection Ireland will go into a full lockdown again soon.

The CMO told Stephen Donnelly that they were concerned Level 3 restrictions wouldn't be enough to stop the spread as he warned the hospitals could see 43 daily admissions by next month.

The letter warned:

  • If the current trend continues Ireland will see between 1,600 and 2,300 cases a day by November 7th.
  • He also warned 43 people would be admitted daily by that date.
  • "Proactive and robust" measures must be taken if there's a hope of seeing a "significant suppression"

Why not Level 3 for NPHET?

The letter said a "graduated" approach wouldn't have a "timely" impact on the spread. They said that going to Level 3 would ultimately result in Level 5 down the line. Going to Level 5 immediately would cause less disruption to non-Covid health and social care services, protect the vulnerable, and "proactively" protect against more disruption to schools and childcare.

Dr Holohan wrote: "The NPHET appreciates how difficult it will be for everyone to return to this level of restrictions, but it is confident that people across Ireland can once again meet this challenge with determination and unity on purpose. Solidarity must continue to be the bedrock of our approach, and the need for intergenerational solidarity has never been greater."

The points the letter outlined as why they were making this recommendation;

  • The total number of cases increased by six percent in the week to 28th September; the number of cases increased by 50 percent in the week to 4th October with 3,063 cases notified.
  • The five day average increased from 310 cases per day to 462 cases per day in the space of a week.
  • The 7-day increased from 43 to 66 per 100,000 people. The 14-day incidence rate increased from 84 to 108 per 100,000.
  • This rate is 119 per 100,000 among 15-24-year-olds.
  • It has increased from 6 per 100,000 in August to 67.3 in October for over 65s.
  • There are 31 "open outbreaks" in nursing homes. Seven of those were reported in the last week. There were been 45 new cases in total in nursing homes last week.
  • There are 25 "open outbreaks" in other vulnerable groups including 10 in Direct Provision, seven within homeless service settings, and 7 in the Traveller community.
  • There was an average of 8 hospital admissions a day on the week up to September 27th. That jumped to 12 last week.
  • There's been an increase in people needing critical care from one per day up to September 29th go two per-day last week. At the peak of the curve earlier this year there were eight critical admissions a day.
  • There are currently 22 in critical care with covid. Of the 281 available beds 243 are currently taken.
  • There were 34 deaths in September compared to four in August.

The backlash

Last night Tanaiste Leo Varadkar made a strong claim on RTE that NPHET didn't have any answers for them if the Level 5 plan didn't work.

He said: "We asked them what do we say to the 400,000 people who would lose their jobs tomorrow and all those businesses. They thought that was a political matter for us.”

"I think what happened in the last couple of days wasn't good for anyone, wasn't good for NPHET, Irish government and wasn't good for the Irish people, many of whom were worried sick today wondering whether they had a job tomorrow, wondering whether they were shutting their business for the last time.

"So we thought that this is not the right way to do things, land something like this on a Sunday night, without prior consultation, without being able to answer very basic questions."

Accountability

After running with the Level 3 plan, Health Minister Stephen Donnelly came in for heavy criticism last night after saying "the virus" would be responsible for any further deaths.

Donnelly's response came after being asked if the government would "hold their hands up" and take responsibility if their decision didn't work and resulted in a Level 5 lockdown in four weeks.

Speaking on Morning Ireland he bypassed the question for a second time saying "“It’s not about who’s right and who’s wrong, it’s about trying to make the best decision we can given the evidence we have.”

Thousands of jobs at risk as pubs and restaurants slam 'devastating' decision

Distraught pub and hotel groups have slammed the decision to move the entire country to Level 3 coronavirus restrictions claiming it will lead to the loss of hundreds of thousands of jobs.

Taoiseach Micheal Martin confirmed this evening that the rest of the nation would move to the higher level of the Living with COVID-19 plan from midnight tomorrow due to surging infection rates.

The restrictions will remain in place until October 27 when they will be reviewed.

The Restaurants Association of Ireland have hit out at the decision saying more than 180,000 workers in the industry will lose their jobs this week as a result.

CEO Adrian Cummins added: “Putting Ireland’s entire restaurant/ hospitality industry on level 3 restrictions is utterly disproportionate, devastating for workers and business owners, plus lacking in evidence based rationale.

"Since June 29, restaurants and hospitality businesses have implemented all of the guidelines and regulations in order to protect staff and customers, while sustaining their business and reacting to the ongoing economic turbulence.

"Since the last of the Government’s new living with Covid framework, there has been absolutely no engagement from NPHET, the HSE or anyone in Government."

Mr Cummins called on the Government to immediately implement an emergency support package for restaurants and cafes affected by tonight's announcement.

He added: "The anger and frustration is palpable across our industry at present and as the representative body for a sector that was first, worst, and continues to be hit most by Covid-19, we need constructive daily dialogue with Government going forward to ensure we exit this lockdown as quickly as possible.

"Public health is and will always be the number one focus. The economy must be a key focus also, businesses are on a cliff edge and this imposed lockdown will close many permanently."

Wet pubs across the country will close again after reopening just two weeks ago.

The Vintners Federation of Ireland has warned that 50,000 jobs are now at risk.

VFI Chief Executive, Padraig Cribben, said: “It’s impossible to overstate the impact this news will have on publicans across the country.

"They now join their colleagues in Donegal and Dublin on Level 3 with all the uncertainty that entails. We also face the grim fact that over 50,000 people will once again have to sign-on this week.

“There now must be an immediate return to the original Pandemic Unemployment Payment for pub staff along with liquidity supports that will allow our members re-establish their businesses once restrictions are removed.

"As a customer-focused sector, disproportionately impacted by restrictions, the very least publicans require is an emergency bailout fund that will support our members until they can return to normal trading conditions.

“There was some hope when pubs reopened two weeks ago they could trade their way to a successful Christmas period but that looks impossible now. We are facing the prospect of being closed for at least nine months of 2020, a fact that Government needs to address.

“While Government say these restrictions will remain in place for three weeks, we have learnt from bitter experience that reopening dates can move at the last minute.

"Our members face another prolonged bout of uncertainty at the worst possible time.

"Next week’s Budget is a crucial opportunity for Government to show its commitment to the pub sector by announcing a series of measures that will restore confidence to a battered trade.”

08:44Gavin O'Callaghan

'Circuit breaker' plan - NPHET vs Government

Last night Tanaiste Leo Varadkar revealed that NPHET's advice to implement Level 5 was a 'circuit' breaker type plan that would have been the first of its kind in Europe.

Varadkar was speaking following the 24 hours of confusion where the emergency team were advising a total lockdown before the government decided to scale it to a Level 3.

The idea was to conduct a "short and sharp" three-week lockdown in a bid to bring the infection rates right down.

Pubs and restaurants would be closed giving the nation "breathing space" in the ongoing pandemic battle.

A ban on socialising with other households would be put in place while public transport also prohibited unless essential.

But surprisingly, NPHET had planned to keep schools open during these four weeks and had not thought about what would happen if no improvement had been seen after the month was up.

Varadkar explained: "They were proposing something called a circuit break. Something that's never been tried anywhere in Europe - what it involves is a short, sharp strict lockdown for 3 or 4 weeks in the hope that it would bring infections down again.

In short, it would see all but essential workplaces closed and restrictions imposed on public spaces.

"We asked for comfort that four weeks would be enough. They weren't able to give us that comfort."

"We asked is it credible to keep the schools open for those four weeks during a strict lockdown. They thought it was, we weren't so sure."

"We asked them what do we say to the 400,000 people who would lose their jobs tomorrow and all those businesses. They thought that was a political matter for us.”

"I think what happened in the last couple of days wasn't good for anyone, wasn't good for NPHET, Irish government and wasn't good for the Irish people, many of whom were worried sick today wondering whether they had a job tomorrow, wondering whether they were shutting their business for the last time.

"So we thought that this is not the right way to do things, land something like this on a Sunday night, without prior consultation, without being able to answer very basic questions."
