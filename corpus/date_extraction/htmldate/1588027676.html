Coronavirus: How these Bay Area travelers got stranded in Bolivia

Repatriation flights from South America can cost thousands of dollars

By Leonardo Castañeda | lcastaneda@bayareanewsgroup.com | Bay Area News Group
PUBLISHED: April 24, 2020 at 9:06 a.m. | UPDATED: April 24, 2020 at 9:26 a.m.

More than a month after the coronavirus pandemic upended international travel, thousands of American citizens are still stuck abroad, navigating high prices to get home and travel restrictions that can make it almost impossible to get to an airport.

Piyum Moghadam, a Palo Alto business consultant, managed to book a flight this week back from La Paz, Bolivia after a month under strict quarantine. Now, he’s spending his time trying to help others return home to the United States.

“There are at least 60 to 100 more people who continue to be stuck in Bolivia,” Moghadam said. “Right now all of the people who were able to return to the states are trying to help those people who are still stranded.”

Officials at the U.S. Department of State said this week that 17,000 American citizens have enrolled in a repatriation program, although they cautioned some might have been signed up by family members and don’t intend to leave whatever country they’re in. As of Thursday, the State Department has brought back 65,565 Americans from 123 countries. About 40 percent have come home on commercial flights arranged in partnership with the State Department and embassies abroad, an official said.

Moghadam was able to catch one of those flights earlier this week, after passing up two previous opportunities. At the time, he said, there had been only a few dozen COVID-19 cases in Bolivia and he had been planning an extended stay anyway. He started a five-month sabbatical trip in February.

Moghadam said he paid about $2,000 for his flight, organized by the U.S. Embassy in Bolivia through a travel agency. He was picked up by a bus in La Paz at 1 a.m. Tuesday and arrived in Miami at about 8 p.m., after three stops to pick up more passengers, two in Bolivia and one, unannounced, in Puerto Rico. From there, he was able to book a cheap flight to San Francisco.

He’s joined a group of American travelers advocating for those left behind, many of whom were planning months-long trips and no longer have jobs to come back to.

“It’s time to come home,” Moghadam, who is now home under quarantine, said. “But you nevertheless are balancing that with a $2,000 price tag, which is a pretty big price tag to pay to come home and not have a reliable source of income.”

The cost of living in Bolivia could be as low as $4 to $7 a day, he said, but many of the Americans still there worry what will happen if they get sick, particularly if they don’t speak Spanish. Dr. William Walters, deputy chief medical officer at the Bureau of Medical Services, said in a briefing every individual has to decide for themselves if it’s safer to shelter in place abroad or to take the risk of going to an airport and being in a confined airplane, with the risk of infection that entails. He cautioned people in countries with few cases but weak healthcare systems that it might be better to come back while they can.

One of the few airlines doing repatriation flights in South America has been Eastern Airlines, an eight-airplane U.S. company relaunched earlier this year. CEO Steve Harfst said they’ve helped repatriate more than 11,000 people through flights from Bolivia, Argentina, Peru and more.

“It’s literally been sometimes 36-hour notice, so it’s really been an amazing effort,” he said, adding that they do no advertising for the flights but let the relevant embassy alert Americans trying to come home.

Harfst said even though Easterrn’s focus is on helping people come home, the fares are higher than usual in part because they’re flying an empty airplane to each country so passengers are in effect paying for the cost of a round trip ticket.

“But I understand the sticker shock when people see it for the first time,” he said.

A spokesperson for Sen. Kamala Harris, D-California, said her office is working to bring back 100 state residents from abroad and is raising “serious concerns about recent reports of unusually high-priced commercial flights for Americans trying to repatriate back home, and facing financial hardships.” Harris joined several other senators in a letter calling on airlines to provide cash refunds for flights canceled because of coronavirus.

Among those still left in Bolivia are Dylan Tinsley, a mechanical engineer from San Jose, and Hannah Phalen, a recent University of California, Berkeley graduate. They flew to Chile and bought a camper in January, the start of what was going to be an eight-month road trip through Central and South America that would end in August when Phalen would start graduate school. In Bolivia, they picked up a light brown, floppy-eared dog called Capo, right before the country shut down. And for the past month, they’ve been in a campsite just outside La Paz, where they have a kitchen, bathrooms and access to WiFi.

“We can only leave the campsite one day a week under order of law because the whole country is on quarantine,” Tinsley said.

On Wednesdays, Tinsley walks an hour downhill to the nearest grocery store — all buses and taxis are shut down — while Phalen scours smaller nearby markets. They have to be back at camp by noon, and Tinsely said the walk back with all the food in La Paz’s high altitudes can be exhausting. The rest of the time they’re in the living room of the campground’s main building, watching Netflix on their phone, and training Capo while Tinsely applies for jobs in the Bay Area.

They were able to raise about $2,400 from family and friends through GoFundMe for a repatriation flight but the airline said Capo was too big to fly, even after they offered to buy him a ticket.

“We love him but he’s made things pretty tough,” he said. “There’s no way we’re going to leave without him. At this point he’s our child, our dog child.”

For now, a statement on the website of the U.S. Embassy in Bolivia says no additional repatriation flights are “currently contemplated.” Tinsley and Phalen are hoping airports will reopen at the end of the month and they’ll be able to eventually fly home, with Capo.
